Designing an Inventory Reporting Experience That Helps Store Owners Understand and Grow Their Business

My Role

Product Designer

Deliverables

High Fidelity Design

Tools

Figma

Duration

2 Weeks

Overview

Designing for small business owners means designing for clarity, speed, and trust. I was invited to rethink how an inventory mobile app could present key business metrics — not with graphs or dashboards, but in a format that any store owner can understand at a glance..

The Problem

When I joined the project, the team was building an inventory mobile app aimed at simplifying stock management for local store owners. But there was a unique challenge waiting to be solved ; how do you present important sales and inventory insights to users who may not be comfortable interpreting charts, graphs, or dashboards?

Traditionally, store owners in the target market relied heavily on notebooks and spreadsheets to track figures like total sales, restocks, monthly profits, and turnover rates. These figures were often manually calculated, written down, and reviewed weekly or monthly.

The goal was to bring these insights into the app — but in a way that actually made sense to the people using them, helping them make better restocking and other critical business decisions without confusion.
Rather than defaulting to standard bar charts or pie graphs, I took a different approach.

The Process: Listening First

I started by diving into the world of store owners. I wanted to understand their daily routines, how they tracked sales, and what numbers mattered most to them. I spent time in local stores, observing how owners interacted with their data and what tools they used. To understand how store owners used their data, I spent time talking to them directly. I asked questions like:

  • "What do you usually write down at the end of the day or week?"
  • "How do you decide when to restock a product?"
  • "What number tells you whether business is good this month?"

These conversations revealed something simple but powerful: most store owners weren't looking for visual analytics — they just wanted clear numbers and comparisons.

They wanted to see:

  • "How much did I sell this week?"
  • "Is that better than last week?"
  • "What products are making me money?"
  • "Which ones are running low?"

That was the moment it clicked for me: They didn’t need DATA VISUALIZATION. They needed DATA UNDERSTANDING.

The Solution: Real Figures, Real Language

I followed three key design principles:

  • Text First – Numbers are the hero. Graphs are gone.
  • Plain Language – No jargon. Just human-readable insights.
  • Familiar Structure – Mimic the way store owners already think (like flipping through a notebook).

Here’s a preview of what it looks like:

End of Day Report

Summarizes daily performance at a glance — total sales, transactions, and profits, helping store owners review the day in seconds.

Customer Report

Highlights top customers and shows the percentage of new vs returning buyers.

Inventory Report

Tracks turnover rate, total products received/sold, and best/worst-performing items — with low-stock alerts for smart restocking.

Transaction Report

Breaks down all purchases, sales, returns, and transfers in one place — useful for audits and accountability.

Profit Summary

Presents profit margins in clear figures and percentages, with comparisons across weeks or months for growth tracking.

Debt Report

Shows total debt, recovery rate, total recovered, debt count, and highlights long-standing debts helping store owners stay on top of what’s owed and what’s at risk.

Compare Report: Performance at a Glance

We wanted store owners to be able to compare how well their business is doing — whether it’s this month vs last month, or this year vs last year.

So I designed the Compare Report to show real numbers side by side. It displays changes in sales, profit, and turnover rate using actual figures

What I Learned

The Feedback That Made My Week 😌:

“This feels like I’m just reading my notebook, but faster.”
“I don’t need to call my son to explain what’s going on anymore.” 
“Now I actually know if I’m making progress.”

That’s the win right there.

Conclusion

Designing for people means meeting them where they are. This project taught me that good design isn’t always about making things pretty, it’s about making things make sense.

Sometimes the best experience is the one that feels like a conversation with your notebook .
